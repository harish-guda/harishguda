I am writing this post as I complete my first quarter of teaching as an Assistant Professor. I taught the core Operations and Supply Chain Management to FT MBAs.1 As I went through this process, I realized that many new instructors might have similar needs in the various courses they teach. For example, a template for a syllabus, an exam, a homework, or even PowerPoint presentations.

While there was an abundance of such resources online, it is hard for a user to try out everything to weed out the bad ones from the good ones. I indulged in this costly search, so you don’t have to.

Here are some of these resources (see Github repo).

  • Exam/Homework Template (LaTeX): While there are many LaTeX templates for exams, I liked this particular template (borrowed and modified from Philip Hirschhorn; Thank you, Philip!). Two features are the points per question calculated on the front page, and the bonus questions.

  • Instruction Slides Template (PowerPoint): If you are not teaching a technical (i.e., notation-intensive) course, and therefore unlikely to use Beamer, I highly recommend PowerPoint. I try to minimize white space on the top, bottom, left and right margins. Many students have commented that the template I use is extremely readable.

    • Pro-tip: I upload two versions of slides for every lecture to the students’ LMS portal (e.g., ASU uses Canvas) while teaching:
      • A “before-class” version and
      • an “after-class” version.
    • I intentionally leave whitespaces/blanks to key answers/issues/aspects in the before-class version. This allows me to have a discussion with my students. I then fill these blanks up during instruction2 and then upload the after-class version.

  • Syllabus Template (LaTeX): Each department/institution has unique requirements for a syllabus. I provide a general-purpose template for a syllabus. Please add any required content to the template.

    • Pro-tip: I recommend you to list the schedule for your course not by date but by a session index (e.g., session 1, 2, etc.).
    • This allows you to make minimal adjustments over multiple terms if the core content remains the same. For instance, I plan to adjust the case studies in my course over the next few years but the key topics remain the same.

  1. My students were absolute gems and I wish every instructor gets to teach the type of students I taught last quarter.↩︎
